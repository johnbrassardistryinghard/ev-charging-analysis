Key Findings

What actually drives
EV charging prices?

Three models were estimated with progressively richer specifications. The results challenge the intuitive assumption that oil and gas prices are the primary driver of EV charging costs.

01

Commodity prices explain almost nothing

WTI oil prices, natural gas prices, and CPI inflation together explain less than 1% of state-level electricity price variation (Adj. R² = 0.01). The link between crude oil and EV charging costs is far weaker than commonly assumed.

02

Demand and seasonality are the dominant drivers

Adding per-capita electricity consumption and seasonal controls jumps explanatory power to 40% (Adj. R² = 0.40). Summer and winter demand surges drive electricity prices up significantly more than global commodity movements.

03

Market structure adds meaningful signal

Whether a state has a regulated or deregulated electricity market adds incremental explanatory power, pushing the model to Adj. R² = 0.43. Deregulated markets show distinct pricing dynamics compared to regulated utility states.

Methodology

How the analysis
was constructed

All data was sourced from public government APIs and official publications. No data was manually estimated or interpolated.

Data Sources

  • EIA API v2: monthly residential electricity prices + sales by state
  • FRED API: WTI crude oil price (WTISPLC), monthly 2021-2023
  • FRED API: Henry Hub natural gas spot price (MHHNGSP)
  • FRED API: CPI (CPIAUCSL) — YoY inflation rate computed
  • U.S. Census Bureau: 2020 state population for per-capita calc
  • Electrify America: public pass member pricing by state

Variables

  • Dependent: state residential electricity price (cents/kWh)
  • WTI crude oil price ($/barrel)
  • Henry Hub natural gas price ($/MMBtu)
  • Year-over-year CPI inflation rate (%)
  • Per-capita electricity consumption (kWh/resident)
  • Season (Winter / Spring / Summer / Fall)
  • Market structure (regulated vs. deregulated)

Econometric Approach

  • OLS regression with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors (HC3)
  • Two-way panel fixed effects tested (state FE + time FE)
  • Panel FE dropped: EA price has no within-state time variation
  • Model selection via AIC (Akaike Information Criterion)
  • Progressive variable addition to isolate marginal contributions
  • All models estimated on balanced panel: n=50, T=36, N=1,800

Tools Used

  • R Studio — primary analysis environment
  • tidyverse — data wrangling and pipeline
  • fredr — FRED API integration
  • httr / jsonlite — EIA API calls
  • plm — panel data modeling
  • lmtest / sandwich — robust standard errors
  • ggplot2 — data visualization
